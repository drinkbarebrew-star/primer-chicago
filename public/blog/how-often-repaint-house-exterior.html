How Often Should You Repaint Your House Exterior in Chicago?

May 28, 2026 8 min read

Chicago is one of the toughest cities in America for exterior paint. Between bone-dry winter cold that drops below zero, spring rainstorms that soak siding for days, brutal summer UV, and the constant freeze-thaw cycling that cracks and peels even the best coatings, your home's exterior takes a beating that most paint timelines simply don't account for. If you've ever wondered when it's actually time to repaint, the answer depends on your siding material, the quality of the last paint job, and how well your home handles moisture.

Here's a comprehensive breakdown so you can plan ahead, budget properly, and avoid the expensive damage that comes from waiting too long.

General Repainting Timelines by Siding Material

Not all siding ages the same way. The material your home is clad in determines your baseline repainting schedule before any Chicago-specific factors are considered.

Wood siding has the shortest lifespan at 5 to 7 years between paint jobs. Wood is porous, absorbs moisture, and expands and contracts with temperature swings. In Chicago, you're often looking at the lower end of that range, especially on south and west-facing walls that take the most UV and weather exposure.

Brick is the most durable substrate, and painted brick can last 15 to 20 years before needing a fresh coat. However, painting brick is a one-way decision. Once painted, the brick requires ongoing maintenance because moisture can become trapped beneath the paint film. If the original paint job was done with a breathable masonry paint like Sherwin-Williams Loxon or a mineral silicate system, you'll get the full 15 to 20 years. A standard latex over bare brick will likely fail much sooner.

Vinyl and aluminum siding can go 10 or more years without repainting, though the color will fade well before that. If your vinyl siding has faded from forest green to sage, a fresh coat of exterior-grade paint formulated for vinyl (which stays flexible and doesn't trap heat) can bring it back without the cost of replacement.

Stucco needs attention every 5 to 6 years. Stucco is prone to hairline cracking, and once moisture infiltrates those cracks, the paint fails rapidly. Elastomeric coatings are the gold standard for stucco because they stretch to bridge small cracks as they form.

Fiber cement (HardiePlank) is increasingly common on Chicago homes and lasts 10 to 15 years between paint jobs. The factory-applied ColorPlus finish from James Hardie carries a 15-year warranty, but field-painted fiber cement may need attention sooner.

Chicago-Specific Factors That Accelerate Paint Deterioration

Those national averages assume moderate climates. Chicago is not moderate. Several local factors push your repainting timeline earlier than what you'll read in generic home improvement articles.

Freeze-thaw cycling is the single biggest paint killer in Chicago. When moisture penetrates behind the paint film, whether through a hairline crack, a failed caulk joint, or a poorly flashed window, it freezes, expands, and physically pushes the paint away from the substrate. A single winter can produce dozens of freeze-thaw cycles, and each one widens the gap between paint and surface. By spring, you see bubbling, peeling, and flaking that wasn't there in October.

Lake Michigan's influence adds humidity and salt-carrying winds that accelerate corrosion on metal components and stress paint adhesion, particularly on homes within a mile or two of the lakefront. Neighborhoods like Lincoln Park, Lakeview, and Edgewater see faster exterior paint degradation than inland areas like Logan Square or Irving Park.

Intense summer UV on south and west-facing walls causes colors to fade and the paint's binder to break down, producing that chalky residue you feel when you run your hand across faded siding. Dark colors fade fastest because they absorb more UV energy, which is why many Chicago homeowners end up repainting their south-facing walls a full cycle before the rest of the house.

Temperature extremes stress paint films constantly. Chicago regularly sees a 100-degree swing between winter lows and summer highs. Paint that can't flex with that range will crack.

7 Signs Your Exterior Needs Repainting

Don't wait for the calendar. Your house will tell you when it's time. Walk around your home every spring and look for these warning signs.

1. Peeling or flaking paint. The most obvious sign. If paint is lifting away from the surface in sheets or curls, moisture has compromised adhesion and no amount of spot repair will fix it. The affected area needs to be scraped, primed, and repainted.

2. Cracking. Hairline cracks (called "checking") in a grid-like pattern mean the paint has become brittle and lost its flexibility. This is common on older oil-based paint that wasn't topcoated with a flexible acrylic latex.

3. Fading. When your home's color has noticeably shifted, especially unevenly between sun-exposed and shaded sides, the UV has degraded the pigment. Fading is more than cosmetic because it indicates the binder is also breaking down.

4. Chalking. Run your hand across the siding. If it comes away with a powdery residue, the paint's binder has deteriorated and the pigment particles are loose on the surface. Some chalking is normal on flat paints, but heavy chalking means it's time.

5. Mildew or mold growth. Black or green spots, especially on north-facing walls that stay damp longer, indicate that the paint's mildewcide has been depleted and moisture is lingering on the surface. Power washing can remove surface mold, but it'll return unless you repaint with a mildew-resistant coating.

6. Bare or exposed wood. If you can see raw wood through worn paint, you're past due. Exposed wood absorbs water like a sponge, leading to rot, insect damage, and structural problems that cost far more than a paint job.

7. Caulk failure. Cracked, shrunk, or missing caulk around windows, doors, and trim joints means water is entering your wall assembly. Recaulking is part of any proper exterior repaint, and failing caulk is often the first domino that leads to broader paint failure.

How Paint Quality Affects Longevity

The difference between a 5-year paint job and a 10-year paint job often comes down to the paint itself. Not all exterior coatings are equal, and in Chicago's demanding climate, premium paint pays for itself.

Benjamin Moore Aura Exterior is our go-to recommendation. Its Color Lock technology resists fading significantly longer than standard exterior paints, and its 100% acrylic formula stays flexible through extreme temperature swings. A gallon costs roughly $75 to $85, compared to $35 to $45 for a builder-grade exterior latex, but that extra $40 per gallon adds 3 to 5 years of life to the finish.

Sherwin-Williams Duration Exterior is another excellent option with PermaLast technology that provides a thick, durable film in fewer coats. For homes in exposed lakefront locations, Duration's moisture resistance is particularly valuable.

Budget paints contain less resin (the binder that holds pigment to the surface and provides flexibility) and more fillers. They go on thin, fade fast, and become brittle within a few Chicago winters. We've seen budget exterior paint jobs fail in under 3 years on south-facing wood siding.

Prep Work: The Hidden Key to Paint Longevity

Even the best paint will fail if applied over a poorly prepared surface. Professional exterior prep in Chicago typically includes power washing to remove dirt, mildew, and chalky residue; scraping and sanding all loose or peeling paint; spot-priming bare wood or patchy areas; recaulking all joints, seams, and penetrations; and repairing or replacing any rotted wood before a single coat of finish paint goes on.

Cutting corners on prep is the most common reason exterior paint jobs fail early. A crew that shows up and starts spraying paint over dirty, peeling siding is giving you a 2-year job at best. Proper prep accounts for 60 to 70 percent of the labor on any exterior repaint.

South vs. North Facing: Which Sides Deteriorate Faster?

Your home's orientation matters more than most homeowners realize. South and west-facing walls take the brunt of UV exposure and afternoon heat, causing faster fading and binder breakdown. North-facing walls stay cooler and damper, making them prone to mildew growth and slower drying after rain. East-facing walls tend to fare best because they get gentle morning sun that dries dew without the intense UV of afternoon exposure.

Many Chicago homeowners find that their south wall needs repainting a full 2 to 3 years before the north wall. A good painting contractor can assess each elevation independently and sometimes recommend a phased approach to save money.

The Cost of Waiting Too Long

Exterior paint is not just cosmetic. It's your home's first line of defense against water intrusion. When paint fails and moisture reaches the substrate, the repair costs escalate quickly. Wood rot repair on window sills and corner boards runs $200 to $600 per area. Replacing rotted siding sections can cost $1,000 to $3,000 depending on the material and extent. Water damage to sheathing and framing behind the siding can run into five figures.

A professional exterior paint job in Chicago typically costs $4,000 to $12,000 depending on home size and condition. That's a fraction of the structural repair bill you'll face if you let paint failure go unchecked for years.

How to Extend Your Exterior Paint Life

Between full repaints, several maintenance practices can significantly extend your paint's lifespan.

Annual power washing removes dirt, pollen, and mildew that degrade the paint film. A gentle wash at 1,500 PSI with a wide fan tip won't damage paint but will keep your siding clean and your finish intact.

Touch up damage immediately. When you spot a chip, scratch, or small peeling area, clean it, prime it, and touch it up before moisture can infiltrate. A $15 quart of matching paint and 30 minutes of effort can prevent a $5,000 problem.

Maintain your gutters. Overflowing or leaking gutters dump water directly onto siding, accelerating paint failure in concentrated areas. Clean gutters twice a year and repair any leaks promptly.

Trim vegetation back from siding. Bushes and trees that touch your house trap moisture against the paint and provide a highway for insects. Keep a 12-inch clearance between any vegetation and your siding.

Recaulk proactively. Don't wait for caulk to completely fail. If you see shrinkage or cracking in caulk joints around windows and trim, remove the old caulk and apply fresh sealant. High-quality polyurethane or siliconized acrylic caulk lasts 10 to 20 years.
